All-grain. Proper ingredients. No shortcuts. The kind of beer people ask if you're going to bottle.

Sunday Session Pale Ale

Bright and approachable. Mosaic and Citra late additions give you tropical fruit and pine without punishing the bitterness. The kind of beer you can drink all afternoon and still have a conversation worth having.

Grapefruit on the nose. Light malt backbone. Clean, dry finish that keeps you coming back. Best at 38°F in a proper pint glass.

Imperial Stout · 9.2% ABV · 55 IBU
Named for Hank's home county. Roasted barley, oats, dark chocolate malt. The kind of stout that earns the word serious. Best in winter. Best with good company. Best in a chair you don't plan to leave.

Dark chocolate and espresso upfront. Vanilla and oak in the middle. Long, warming finish. Winston approves. That is not nothing.

The Process

How It's Made

All-grain, start to finish. No extract. No shortcuts.

01
Mash
Grains steeped at precise temperature. Enzymes convert starches to fermentable sugars. 60–90 minutes of patience.
02
Lauter
Sweet wort separated from spent grain. Sparged with hot water to rinse every last sugar from the grain bed.
03
Boil
60–90 minute rolling boil. Hops added in timed additions. Bittering, flavor, aroma — each hop addition has a job.
04
Cool & Pitch
Wort chilled rapidly to fermentation temperature. Yeast pitched. The most important 30 minutes in the whole process.
05
Ferment
7–21 days depending on the style. Temperature controlled. Gravity monitored by Tilt Pro hydrometer — Hank watches it live.
06
Condition & Package
Cold crash, dry hop if warranted, keg or bottle. Carbonation set. The waiting begins. Worth it every time.

The Pit

Low. Slow. Right.

Live fire, proper smoke, and the patience to do it the way it's meant to be done. Hickory and post oak. No shortcuts.

🥩
Central Texas Brisket
Packer Brisket · 16-Hour Cook
Salt and pepper. Post oak. 225°F until she's ready — and not a minute before. Bark like leather, fat rendered clean, the flat moist enough to eat without sauce. A proper brisket is a full day's commitment. It's worth every hour.
Post Oak225°F16 hrsSalt + Pepper Only
🍖
St. Louis Spare Ribs
Spare Ribs · 5-Hour Cook · 3-2-1 Method
Trimmed St. Louis style. Dry rubbed the night before. Three hours of smoke, two hours wrapped, one hour back on the grate. The result bends without breaking, pulls clean from the bone, and disappears faster than anything else on the table.
Hickory250°F5 hrs3-2-1
🐖
Pulled Pork
Bone-In Pork Shoulder · 12-Hour Cook
The crowd feeder. Eight-pound shoulder, simple rub, apple wood smoke, twelve hours at 225°F. Pulled while hot, piled high. The shoulder is forgiving in a way brisket isn't — but that doesn't mean you rush it. You never rush it.
Apple Wood225°F12 hrsBone-In

Spatchcocked flat on the grate. Cherry wood for a mahogany skin and a subtle sweetness. Brined overnight. The fastest cook in the rotation but the one people always seem the most impressed by — something about the whole bird makes it feel like an occasion.

The Legendary Cheesecake
The One People Drive For

Sourdough Country Loaf
Long Ferment · Dutch Oven Bake

The Still House

What Hank Makes

Traditional Appalachian distilling knowledge applied to small-batch craft production. Hank supervises. Hank always supervises.

🌽
Traditional Corn Whiskey
80% Corn · 10% Malted Barley · 10% Rye
Hank's granddaddy's recipe, interpreted through a modern grain bill and proper cuts. Unaged, clear, and honest. The kind of spirit that shows you what the grain actually tastes like without the barrel getting in the way.
Hank's Notes

"The barrel's for covering up mistakes. Good corn whiskey doesn't need covering up."

🪵
Oak-Rested Corn Whiskey
Same Recipe · 6-Month Charred Oak Rest
The same corn whiskey, rested in a 3-liter charred American oak barrel for six months. The color deepens. Vanilla and caramel emerge. The grain is still there — the barrel just had a conversation with it.
Winston's Notes

"Not bourbon. Not trying to be. It's its own thing, and its own thing is quite good."

Apple Brandy
Estate Apple Wash · Double Distilled
Fresh apple juice fermented and double-distilled through a copper pot still. Light, fruited, elegant. The spirit that surprises people who thought they didn't like brandy. They didn't like bad brandy. This is not bad brandy.
Tasting Notes

"Fresh apple, stone fruit, a hint of floral. Long, clean finish. Best neat at room temperature."
